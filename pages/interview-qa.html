Roles & What Each Interview Tests

Know which game you're playing

The same company interviews an "AI Engineer" and an "ML Engineer" very differently. Read the JD, map it to one of these four buckets, and weight your prep accordingly.

AI Engineer

Builds on top of LLMs. Tested on: RAG, chunking, embeddings, prompt engineering, agents, tool calling, evals, latency/cost trade-offs. Light on classical ML math.

ML Engineer

Trains and ships models. Tested on: classical ML depth, deep learning, feature engineering, model serving, drift, pipelines. Coding rounds are heavier.

AI/ML Engineer (hybrid)

Startup-style role: everything above, shallower each. Tested on breadth — expect one classical question, one DL question, one GenAI question, one system design.

Data Scientist

Insight and experimentation. Tested on: statistics, A/B testing, p-values, SQL, metrics design, regression/classification intuition, communicating results.

Surface "Walk me through a project" — what are they really asking?

They want to hear: (1) the business problem, (2) why you chose your approach over alternatives, (3) how you measured success, (4) what failed and what you did about it. Use a 2-minute structure: problem → data → approach → metric → result → what you'd improve. The follow-up questions will drill into whichever part you sound least confident about.

ML Fundamentals

Surface What is machine learning, in one sentence?

Machine learning is teaching a computer to find patterns from data instead of writing explicit rules — the algorithm adjusts its internal parameters to minimize its mistakes on examples, then applies those learned patterns to new, unseen data.

Surface Supervised vs unsupervised vs reinforcement learning?

Supervised: data comes with labels (answer keys) — predict house prices, classify spam. Unsupervised: no labels — find structure yourself, like clustering customers or reducing dimensions. Reinforcement: an agent learns by trial and error, getting rewards or penalties — game playing, robotics, and RLHF for aligning LLMs.

Surface What is overfitting and underfitting?

Overfitting: the model memorizes the training data — including its noise — so it aces training but fails on new data. Underfitting: the model is too simple to capture the pattern at all, so it does badly on both. You detect overfitting by a large gap between training and validation performance. Fixes: more data, regularization, simpler model, early stopping, dropout (in neural nets).

Mid Explain the bias-variance tradeoff simply.

Bias = error from being too simple (wrong assumptions — underfitting). Variance = error from being too sensitive to the specific training data (overfitting). Total error = bias² + variance + irreducible noise. Simple models: high bias, low variance. Complex models: low bias, high variance. The art of ML is finding the sweet spot — usually via cross-validation and regularization.

Surface Why do we split data into train / validation / test?

Train: the model learns from this. Validation: used to tune hyperparameters and compare models — the model never trains on it. Test: touched exactly once at the very end for an honest estimate of real-world performance. If you tune on the test set, its score becomes optimistic and meaningless — that's a form of leakage.

Mid What is K-fold cross-validation, and what is the K here?

Split the data into K equal parts (folds). Train on K−1 folds, validate on the remaining one, rotate K times so every fold gets a turn as validation, then average the K scores. K here is simply the number of folds — typically 5 or 10. Higher K = more reliable estimate but more compute. K = n (one sample per fold) is called leave-one-out CV. Use stratified K-fold for imbalanced classification so each fold keeps the class ratio.

Surface Classification vs regression?

Classification predicts a category (spam / not spam, cat / dog). Regression predicts a continuous number (price, temperature). The giveaway in an interview problem: "how much / how many" → regression; "which one / yes-no" → classification.

Mid Parametric vs non-parametric models?

Parametric: fixed number of parameters regardless of data size (linear regression, logistic regression, naive Bayes). Fast, needs less data, but limited flexibility. Non-parametric: complexity grows with the data (KNN, decision trees, kernel SVM). More flexible, but needs more data and can overfit. LLMs are technically parametric — huge but fixed parameter count.

Mid Generative vs discriminative models?

Discriminative models learn the boundary between classes — they model P(y|x) directly (logistic regression, SVM). Generative models learn how the data itself is distributed — P(x, y) or P(x) — so they can generate new samples (naive Bayes, GANs, diffusion models, LLMs). LLMs are generative: they model the probability of the next token and can sample new text.

Depth Probe What is the curse of dimensionality?

As the number of features grows, data becomes exponentially sparse — every point ends up far from every other point, so "nearest neighbor" stops meaning anything and models need exponentially more data to generalize. Distance-based methods (KNN, K-means) suffer most. Fixes: feature selection, PCA/dimensionality reduction, or learned embeddings that compress meaning into fewer dimensions.

Mid What are semi-supervised and self-supervised learning?

Semi-supervised: you have a little labeled data and a lot of unlabeled data — train on the labeled part, use the model to pseudo-label the rest, retrain (self-training, label propagation). Self-supervised: no human labels at all — invent the labels from the data itself: mask a word and predict it (BERT), predict the next token (GPT), or pull augmented views of the same image together (contrastive learning, SimCLR). Self-supervised pretraining is literally how every modern LLM gets its knowledge — worth saying that out loud in an interview.

Classical ML

Deep dive available

Full derivations live in Pillar 2 · Classical ML.

Surface How does linear regression work, simply?

It draws the best straight line (or hyperplane) through the data by minimizing the sum of squared differences between predictions and actual values. "Best" is found either with a closed-form formula (normal equation) or gradient descent. Output is continuous, so it's for regression problems.

Surface Why is logistic regression called "regression" if it classifies?

Because it regresses a probability: it fits a linear function, then squashes the output through a sigmoid to get a value between 0 and 1 — P(y=1|x). You then threshold that probability (usually at 0.5) to classify. It's trained with cross-entropy (log) loss, not squared error, because that keeps the loss convex and the gradients healthy.

Mid How does KNN work, and how do you choose the K in KNN?

K-Nearest Neighbors classifies a new point by looking at the K closest training points and taking a majority vote (or averaging, for regression). There's no training phase — it memorizes the data. K here = how many neighbors get to vote. Small K (1–3): very flexible, sensitive to noise (overfits). Large K: smoother, but can blur class boundaries (underfits). Choose K via cross-validation; use an odd K for binary classification to avoid tie votes. Always scale features first, since KNN is distance-based.

Mid How does K-means work, and how do you choose the K in K-means?

K-means groups data into K clusters: (1) place K random centroids, (2) assign each point to its nearest centroid, (3) move each centroid to the mean of its assigned points, (4) repeat until stable. K here = the number of clusters you ask for — the algorithm can't discover it. To choose K: the elbow method (plot within-cluster variance vs K, pick where the improvement flattens) or the silhouette score. Business context often decides too.

Surface KNN vs K-means — people confuse them. What's the difference?

Completely different algorithms that share a letter. KNN is supervised (needs labels) and K = number of neighbors that vote on a prediction. K-means is unsupervised (no labels) and K = number of clusters to form. KNN predicts for new points; K-means organizes existing points into groups.

Surface How does a decision tree decide where to split?

At each node it tries every feature and threshold, and picks the split that makes the resulting groups most "pure" — measured by Gini impurity or entropy / information gain. It repeats recursively until leaves are pure or a stopping rule hits (max depth, min samples). Trees overfit easily, which is why we prune them or use ensembles.

Mid Bagging vs boosting — what's the difference?

Bagging (Random Forest): train many models in parallel on random bootstrap samples, then average/vote. Reduces variance — great for unstable models like deep trees. Boosting (XGBoost, LightGBM): train models sequentially, each one focusing on the errors of the previous ones. Reduces bias — turns weak learners into a strong one. Boosting usually wins on accuracy for tabular data; bagging is more robust to noise.

Mid Why does Random Forest also randomize features, not just rows?

If one feature is very strong, every bagged tree would split on it first and all trees would look alike — averaging correlated trees barely reduces variance. By giving each split only a random subset of features, trees are forced to be different from each other (decorrelated), and averaging diverse trees cuts variance much more.

Surface What is XGBoost and why does everyone use it for tabular data?

XGBoost is gradient boosting engineered to be fast and regularized: it adds trees that correct the current ensemble's errors using gradients of the loss, with built-in L1/L2 regularization, tree pruning, missing-value handling, and parallelized split-finding. On structured/tabular data it (and LightGBM/CatBoost) still routinely beats neural networks while being cheaper to train and tune.

Mid What does an SVM do, and what is the kernel trick in one line?

An SVM finds the separating boundary with the maximum margin — the widest possible gap between classes, defined by the closest points (support vectors). The kernel trick: instead of explicitly mapping data into a higher dimension where it becomes separable, use a kernel function to compute the dot products in that space directly — you get non-linear boundaries at linear cost.

Surface Why is naive Bayes "naive," and why does it still work?

It naively assumes all features are independent given the class — almost never true. But classification only needs the right class to score highest, not correct probabilities, so the wrong-but-consistent estimates still rank classes correctly. It's fast, needs little data, and remains a strong baseline for text classification.

Mid L1 vs L2 regularization — difference in one breath?

Both add a penalty on weight size to the loss to prevent overfitting. L1 (Lasso) penalizes |w| — it pushes weights to exactly zero, doing automatic feature selection. L2 (Ridge) penalizes w² — it shrinks all weights smoothly toward zero but rarely to zero, handling correlated features better. Elastic Net combines both.

Metrics & Evaluation

Surface Explain the confusion matrix in plain words.

A 2×2 table of what your classifier got right and wrong: True Positive (predicted yes, was yes), True Negative (predicted no, was no), False Positive (predicted yes, was no — a false alarm), False Negative (predicted no, was yes — a miss). Every classification metric — precision, recall, F1, accuracy — is computed from these four numbers.

Mid Precision vs recall — and when do you prioritize which?

Precision = of everything I flagged positive, how many were right? TP/(TP+FP). Recall = of all the actual positives out there, how many did I catch? TP/(TP+FN). Prioritize recall when missing a positive is costly: cancer screening, fraud detection. Prioritize precision when false alarms are costly: spam filters, legal document flagging. There's always a tradeoff — moving the decision threshold trades one for the other.

Surface What is F1 score and when do you use it?

F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall: 2PR/(P+R). The harmonic mean punishes imbalance — if either precision or recall is terrible, F1 is terrible, unlike a simple average. Use it when you need one number that respects both, especially on imbalanced data where accuracy lies.

Mid Why is accuracy misleading on imbalanced data?

If 99% of transactions are legit, a model that predicts "legit" for everything scores 99% accuracy while catching zero fraud. On imbalanced problems, report precision/recall/F1 for the minority class, PR-AUC, or use a confusion matrix — never bare accuracy. This is one of the most common interview traps.

Mid What does ROC-AUC actually measure?

The ROC curve plots true-positive rate vs false-positive rate across all thresholds. AUC is the probability that the model ranks a random positive above a random negative — 1.0 is perfect, 0.5 is coin-flip. It's threshold-independent, good for comparing models. Caveat: on heavily imbalanced data, ROC-AUC looks optimistic; PR-AUC is more honest there.

Surface Which metrics for regression, and when?

MAE: average absolute error — robust to outliers, easy to explain in business units. MSE/RMSE: squares errors, so big mistakes hurt much more — use when large errors are disproportionately bad. R²: fraction of variance explained. MAPE: percentage error — intuitive but explodes near zero values.

Depth Probe How do you evaluate an LLM output where there's no single right answer?

Layered approach: (1) Automatic checks where possible — exact match, unit tests for code, regex for format. (2) LLM-as-judge — a stronger model scores outputs against a rubric (correctness, groundedness, tone); calibrate the judge against human labels first. (3) Human evaluation on a sample as ground truth. (4) In production: A/B tests on user behavior (acceptance rate, thumbs up/down). Mention pairwise comparison — judges are more reliable comparing two outputs than scoring one in isolation.

Features & Data Prep

Surface Normalization vs standardization — which and when?

Normalization (min-max): rescale to [0,1]. Good when you need bounded values (neural nets, image pixels) but sensitive to outliers. Standardization (z-score): subtract mean, divide by std — values centered at 0. More robust to outliers; the default for distance-based models (KNN, SVM, K-means) and anything with gradient descent. Tree-based models (RF, XGBoost) don't need scaling at all.

Surface One-hot vs label encoding for categorical features?

Label encoding maps categories to integers — fine for tree models, but linear models will falsely read an order into it. One-hot makes a binary column per category — no fake order, but explodes with high-cardinality features. For many categories, use target encoding (replace category with its mean target value) or learned embeddings.

Mid How do you handle missing values?

First ask why they're missing — random, or informative? Options: (1) drop rows/columns if few and random, (2) impute with median/mode or model-based imputation (KNN, iterative), (3) add a "was_missing" indicator column — often the missingness itself is signal, (4) XGBoost/LightGBM handle missing values natively. Never impute using statistics computed over the full dataset — compute them on train only, or you leak.

Mid What is data leakage, and what's a sneaky example?

Leakage = information from outside the training window sneaking into features, giving unrealistically good offline scores that collapse in production. Sneaky examples: scaling with statistics computed on the whole dataset before splitting; a "days_until_churn" style feature that encodes the answer; duplicate rows landing in both train and test. Rule: build every preprocessing step inside the CV loop, fit on train only.

Mid How do you deal with imbalanced classes?

(1) Fix the metric — use PR-AUC/F1 and tune the decision threshold; often enough. (2) Class weights — penalize minority-class mistakes more in the loss. (3) Resampling — undersample majority or oversample minority (SMOTE synthesizes new minority points). (4) Collect more minority data if possible. And never resample the test set — it must reflect reality.

Mid What is PCA, in simple terms?

PCA finds new axes (principal components) along which the data varies the most, and lets you keep just the top few — compressing many correlated features into a few uncorrelated ones while losing minimal information. Mechanically: eigenvectors of the covariance matrix, ranked by eigenvalue. Uses: visualization, denoising, speeding up training. Caveats: standardize first, components lose human interpretability.

Surface How do you detect and handle outliers?

Detect: box plot / IQR rule (outside 1.5×IQR), z-score > 3, or model-based (Isolation Forest, DBSCAN noise points). Handle: first determine if it's an error (fix or drop) or a real extreme value (keep — it may be the most important signal, e.g. fraud!). Options: cap/winsorize, log-transform skewed features, or use robust models (trees) and robust metrics (MAE).

Statistics for Data Science

Mid What is a p-value, without the textbook jargon?

Assume nothing interesting is going on (the null hypothesis). The p-value is the probability of seeing data at least as extreme as yours just by random chance under that assumption. Small p-value (< 0.05 by convention) = "this would be really surprising if nothing were going on" → reject the null. It is not the probability the null is true — that's the classic misinterpretation interviewers fish for.

Surface Central Limit Theorem — why does everyone care?

Take averages of enough independent samples and those averages follow a normal distribution, no matter how weird the underlying data's distribution is. This is why we can build confidence intervals and run hypothesis tests on means without knowing the true distribution — the bell curve shows up for free. Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 is usually enough.

Mid Type I vs Type II errors?

Type I (false positive): you cry wolf — reject a true null. Its probability is α, your significance level (0.05). Type II (false negative): you miss a real effect. Its probability is β; power = 1−β. They trade off: lowering α makes you stricter and increases misses. Which is worse depends on context — a Type I in medicine approves a useless drug; a Type II kills a genuinely good feature launch.

Mid Walk me through designing an A/B test.

(1) Define one primary metric and guardrail metrics. (2) State the hypothesis and the minimum detectable effect you care about. (3) Compute required sample size from MDE, α (0.05) and power (0.8) — before starting. (4) Randomize users (not sessions), run the full planned duration, no peeking-and-stopping early. (5) Run the significance test, check guardrails and segments, then decide. Bonus: mention novelty effects and multiple-testing corrections.

Surface Correlation vs causation — give an example.

Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths — not because ice cream kills, but because summer drives both (a confounder). Correlation says two things move together; causation says one drives the other. To claim causation you need a randomized experiment (A/B test) or careful causal-inference techniques (difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, propensity matching).

Mid What does a 95% confidence interval actually mean?

If you repeated the experiment many times and built an interval each time, 95% of those intervals would contain the true value. It's a statement about the procedure, not "there's a 95% chance the truth is in this specific interval" — that phrasing belongs to Bayesian credible intervals. Practically: if two groups' CIs for a difference exclude 0, the difference is significant.

Deep Learning

Deep dive available

Surface Why do neural networks need activation functions?

Without them, stacking layers is pointless: a chain of linear layers collapses into one linear layer (a matrix times a matrix is still a matrix). Non-linear activations (ReLU, GELU) between layers let the network bend and compose functions, which is what lets deep nets approximate arbitrarily complex patterns.

Mid Explain backpropagation simply.

Backprop is the chain rule, applied efficiently. Forward pass: compute the prediction and the loss. Backward pass: walk from the loss back through each layer, computing how much each weight contributed to the error (its gradient) by multiplying local derivatives along the way. Then nudge every weight a small step against its gradient (gradient descent). The trick that makes it fast: gradients computed at later layers are reused for earlier layers, so the whole thing costs about as much as one forward pass.

Mid What are vanishing and exploding gradients, and the fixes?

In deep networks, gradients are products of many layer derivatives. If those are mostly < 1, the product shrinks toward zero — early layers stop learning (vanishing). If mostly > 1, it blows up (exploding). Fixes: ReLU-family activations, careful init (He/Xavier), residual/skip connections (gradient highways — the key idea in ResNet and Transformers), normalization layers, and gradient clipping for the exploding case.

Surface What does dropout do, and why does it work?

During training, dropout randomly zeroes a fraction (e.g. 10–50%) of neurons each step, so the network can't rely on any single neuron — it's forced to learn redundant, robust features. It acts like training an ensemble of many thinned networks that share weights. At inference dropout is turned off. It's a regularizer: use it when overfitting, not by default everywhere.

Mid BatchNorm vs LayerNorm — and why do Transformers use LayerNorm?

Both normalize activations to stabilize and speed up training. BatchNorm normalizes each feature across the batch — it depends on batch statistics, so it breaks with tiny batches and behaves differently at inference. LayerNorm normalizes across features within each single example — batch-size independent, same behavior train/test. Transformers use LayerNorm because sequence lengths vary, batches can be small, and each token needs consistent normalization independent of its neighbors.

Mid SGD vs Adam — what's the practical difference?

SGD uses one global learning rate for all weights. Adam keeps a running average of each weight's gradient (momentum) and of its squared gradient, giving every weight its own adaptive learning rate. Adam converges fast with little tuning, which is why it (as AdamW) is the default for Transformers/LLMs. SGD+momentum can generalize slightly better in vision, but needs careful LR scheduling.

Surface Epoch vs batch vs iteration?

Epoch: one full pass over the training set. Batch: the subset of samples processed before one weight update. Iteration: one weight update (one batch). So with 10,000 samples and batch size 100, one epoch = 100 iterations. Larger batch = stabler gradients and better GPU utilization; smaller batch = noisier gradients (which can help generalization) and less memory.

Surface CNN vs RNN — when would you use each?

CNNs exploit spatial locality with sliding filters — images, audio spectrograms, anything grid-like. RNNs/LSTMs process sequences step by step with a hidden state as memory — they were the standard for text and time series until Transformers replaced them almost everywhere. Honest modern answer: CNNs still rule efficient vision; RNNs survive in low-latency streaming and small on-device models; Transformers dominate the rest.

Surface What is transfer learning?

Start from a model trained on a huge general dataset, then adapt it to your task with a little data — either freeze the base and train just a new head, or fine-tune everything with a small learning rate. It works because early layers learn universal features (edges, textures, grammar) that transfer. Every modern LLM workflow is transfer learning: pretrain once, fine-tune/adapt endlessly.

Mid LSTM vs GRU — what's the difference and which do you pick?

Both are gated RNNs that fix vanishing gradients with a memory pathway. LSTM has three gates (forget, input, output) and a separate cell state. GRU simplifies to two gates (update, reset) — fewer parameters, faster to train, often equal accuracy. Rule of thumb: try GRU first for speed on smaller datasets; LSTM sometimes edges ahead on longer sequences. Honest 2026 note: for most new NLP work you'd reach for a Transformer anyway.

Mid What are knowledge distillation and model pruning?

Distillation: train a small "student" model to imitate a big "teacher" — the student learns from the teacher's soft probability outputs (which carry richer information than hard labels). DistilBERT keeps ~97% of BERT's performance at 40% of the size this way. Pruning: delete weights that contribute least (small magnitudes, whole neurons or attention heads), then fine-tune to recover. Both compose with quantization — the standard toolbox for edge/on-device deployment.

Computer Vision

JDs ask for this

Real AI/ML Engineer JDs list Computer Vision alongside LLMs — expect at least one detection/OCR scenario if CV is on the JD.

Surface Image classification vs object detection vs segmentation?

Classification: one label for the whole image ("this is a cat"). Object detection: find where things are — bounding boxes + labels for every object (YOLO, Faster R-CNN). Segmentation: label every pixel — semantic (all "car" pixels) or instance (car #1 vs car #2; Mask R-CNN, SAM). Pick by the question: "what is it" → classify, "where is it" → detect, "exact outline" → segment.

Mid How does YOLO work, and why is it the default for real-time detection?

YOLO ("You Only Look Once") does detection in a single forward pass: the image is divided into a grid, and each cell directly predicts bounding boxes, confidence, and class probabilities — no separate region-proposal stage. One pass = fast enough for video (30+ FPS). Overlapping duplicate boxes are cleaned up with non-max suppression. In practice you fine-tune a pretrained YOLOv8/v11 on 2–5k annotated images.

Mid What are IoU and mAP?

IoU (Intersection over Union): overlap between predicted and true bounding box — area of overlap ÷ area of union, 0 to 1. A detection typically "counts" if IoU ≥ 0.5. mAP (mean Average Precision): the standard detection metric — average precision across recall levels, per class, then averaged over classes. mAP@0.5 uses the 0.5 IoU threshold; mAP@0.5:0.95 (COCO-style) averages over stricter thresholds.

Mid Walk me through an OCR pipeline for scanned invoices.

Four stages: (1) Preprocess — deskew, denoise, binarize (Otsu thresholding) with OpenCV. (2) OCR — Tesseract for clean prints, PaddleOCR/TrOCR for harder cases, or managed services (AWS Textract) which also return table/form structure. (3) Extract fields — regex/NER for fixed formats, or an LLM with structured (JSON) output for messy layouts. (4) Validate — check that totals add up, dates parse, required fields exist; route failures to human review.

Surface What is data augmentation and why does it matter more in CV?

Creating modified copies of training images — flips, rotations, crops, brightness/color shifts, blur, cutout — so the model sees more variation than you actually collected. It's a regularizer and cheap insurance against real-world conditions (lighting, angle, occlusion). Rule: only apply transforms that preserve the label. Libraries: albumentations, torchvision transforms.

Depth Probe How do you deploy a vision model for real-time inference on a production line?

Export the trained model into an optimized runtime: ONNX (portable, framework-agnostic) or TensorRT (NVIDIA-specific — fuses layers, quantizes to FP16/INT8, typically 3–5× faster). Serve it in a Docker container with GPU access (NVIDIA Container Toolkit), fed by the camera stream (RTSP or Kafka). Design decisions: batch frames vs per-frame latency, confidence threshold tuned for recall, and drift monitoring — watch the confidence distribution over time; lighting changes silently degrade vision models.

NLP & Transformers

Deep dive available

Surface What is tokenization and why do LLMs use subwords?

Tokenization splits text into units the model can process. Modern LLMs use subword tokenizers (BPE, WordPiece, SentencePiece). Why subwords? Whole words give huge vocabularies and can't handle unseen words; characters make sequences too long. Subwords are the middle ground: common words stay whole ("the"), rare words split into pieces ("tokeni" + "zation"). Rule of thumb: 1 token ≈ ¾ of an English word.

Surface What are embeddings, in simple terms?

Embeddings turn words/sentences/documents into vectors of numbers where similar meaning = nearby vectors. "King" and "queen" land close together; "king" and "toaster" far apart. Static embeddings (word2vec, GloVe) give one vector per word; contextual embeddings (BERT-style) give a different vector depending on the sentence — "bank" of a river vs a bank account get different vectors.

Mid Explain attention like I'm not a mathematician.

For each word, attention asks: "which other words in this text should I look at to understand this word, and how much?" Mechanically: each token makes a query ("what am I looking for?"), a key ("what do I contain?") and a value ("what do I give you?"). Match queries against keys → weights → weighted sum of values. Multi-head = running several of these lookups in parallel, each learning a different kind of relationship (syntax, coreference, position…).

Mid Why did Transformers replace RNNs?

Three reasons: (1) Parallelism — RNNs must process tokens one at a time; Transformers see the whole sequence at once. (2) Long-range dependencies — in an RNN, information from 500 tokens ago must survive 500 squashing steps; attention connects any two tokens in one hop. (3) Scaling laws — Transformers keep improving predictably as you add data and parameters, which made the GPT recipe possible.

Mid BERT vs GPT — the core architectural difference?

BERT is an encoder: every token attends to both left and right context (bidirectional), trained by masking words and guessing them. Great for understanding tasks — classification, NER, embeddings for retrieval. GPT is a decoder: each token attends only to previous tokens (causal mask), trained to predict the next token. That makes it a text generator — and next-token prediction at scale learns almost everything, which is why decoder-only won the LLM era.

Surface Why do Transformers need positional encodings?

Attention is a bag-of-tokens operation — without position info, "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" look identical. The original paper added sine/cosine waves of different frequencies to each token; modern LLMs use RoPE (rotary embeddings), which rotate query/key vectors by an angle proportional to position so attention naturally depends on relative distance — this also extends better to longer contexts.

Surface What is a context window?

The maximum number of tokens the model can consider at once — its working memory. Everything (system prompt, chat history, retrieved documents, its own output) must fit inside it. Bigger windows cost more: the KV cache grows linearly, and naive attention compute grows quadratically. This constraint is why chunking and RAG exist — you retrieve only the relevant pieces instead of stuffing everything in.

Surface What are Bag of Words and TF-IDF, and are they still relevant?

Bag of Words: represent a document as word counts, ignoring order. TF-IDF weights: term frequency × inverse document frequency — words frequent in this document but rare across the corpus score high. Still relevant: TF-IDF/BM25 powers the sparse half of hybrid search in RAG, is a strong fast baseline for text classification, and wins on exact keyword/ID matching where embeddings fumble.

Mid Explain Word2Vec and its famous property.

Word2Vec learns a vector per word by predicting context: Skip-gram predicts surrounding words from the center word; CBOW does the reverse. The famous property: vector arithmetic captures meaning — king − man + woman ≈ queen. Limitation: vectors are static — "bank" has one vector regardless of context — which is exactly what contextual models (ELMo, BERT) fixed.

Surface Stemming vs lemmatization?

Both reduce words to a base form. Stemming chops suffixes with rules (Porter stemmer): fast but crude — "studies" → "studi". Lemmatization uses a dictionary and part-of-speech: "studies" → "study", "better" → "good"; slower but correct. Modern caveat: LLM pipelines skip both — subword tokenization handles morphology implicitly; these matter for classical NLP and TF-IDF/BM25 preprocessing.

Mid What is Named Entity Recognition and how is it implemented?

NER finds and classifies entities in text — people, organizations, locations, dates ("Apple [ORG] hired Tim [PER] in March [DATE]"). Implementations by era: rules → CRFs → BiLSTM-CRF → today's standard, a fine-tuned BERT with a token-classification head using BIO tags. Off-the-shelf: spaCy for speed, or an LLM with structured output for zero-shot flexibility. Production uses: PII detection/redaction (spaCy + Presidio), invoice field extraction, metadata enrichment for RAG chunks.

Generative AI & LLMs

Surface What is an LLM actually doing when it "answers"?

Predicting the next token, over and over. Given all the text so far, it outputs a probability for every token in its vocabulary, one is sampled, appended, and the loop repeats. All the apparent reasoning, style, and knowledge emerge from doing this prediction extremely well after training on trillions of tokens. There's no database lookup and no explicit logic engine — which explains both its flexibility and its failure modes (hallucination).

Surface Pretraining vs fine-tuning vs RLHF — the three stages?

(1) Pretraining: next-token prediction on massive raw text → a base model with knowledge but no manners. (2) Supervised fine-tuning (SFT): train on curated instruction→response pairs → it learns to be an assistant. (3) Preference alignment (RLHF/DPO): humans rank outputs, the model is optimized toward preferred ones → helpful, safer, better-formatted answers. Stages 2–3 cost a tiny fraction of stage 1.

Mid What do temperature, top-k and top-p do?

They control sampling randomness. Temperature rescales the probability distribution: <1 sharpens it (safe, repetitive, good for code/facts), >1 flattens it (creative, riskier), 0 = always pick the top token (greedy). Top-k: only sample among the k most likely tokens (K = how many candidates). Top-p (nucleus): sample from the smallest set of tokens whose probabilities add up to p (e.g. 0.9) — the candidate pool adapts to the model's confidence. Production default: temperature ~0–0.3 for factual tasks, ~0.7–1.0 for creative ones.

Mid Why do LLMs hallucinate, and how do you reduce it?

Because the model is trained to produce plausible next tokens, not verified ones — when it lacks knowledge, the most statistically plausible continuation is a confident-sounding invention. Reductions: (1) RAG — ground answers in retrieved documents and instruct "answer only from context," (2) allow and reward "I don't know" in prompts, (3) lower temperature, (4) verification layers — self-check prompts, citations validated programmatically, LLM-as-judge grading groundedness, (5) fine-tune on domain data. You manage hallucination; you don't eliminate it.

Surface Zero-shot vs few-shot vs chain-of-thought prompting?

Zero-shot: just ask ("Classify this review's sentiment"). Few-shot: include 2–5 worked examples in the prompt — the model imitates the pattern; the single cheapest accuracy boost for format-sensitive tasks. Chain-of-thought: ask the model to reason step-by-step before answering — dramatically improves math/logic/multi-step tasks. Modern "reasoning models" bake CoT in, doing it internally before answering.

Mid Fine-tuning vs RAG — how do you decide?

Ask what you're trying to change. Knowledge that updates (docs, policies, product data) → RAG: update the index, get citations, no retraining. Behavior/style/format (tone, domain jargon, strict JSON output) → fine-tuning: it bakes the behavior in. They compose: many production systems fine-tune for format+style AND use RAG for facts. Start with prompting → RAG → fine-tune only when the first two plateau.

Mid What is LoRA and why is it everywhere?

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) freezes the base model and trains only tiny low-rank matrices added beside the weight matrices: instead of updating a d×d matrix, learn two skinny matrices A (d×r) and B (r×d) with rank r ≈ 8–64, so ΔW = BA. You train <1% of the parameters — one GPU instead of a cluster. QLoRA pushes further: quantize the frozen base to 4-bit, train LoRA on top — fine-tune a 70B model on a single high-memory GPU.

Surface What is quantization?

Storing model weights in fewer bits — FP16 → INT8 → 4-bit — so the model needs 2–4× less memory and runs faster, with small accuracy loss. It's the main lever for running big models on small hardware (a 7B model in 4-bit fits in ~4 GB). Formats: GGUF (llama.cpp, CPU-friendly), AWQ/GPTQ (GPU). Trade-off: aggressive quantization degrades quality, hitting reasoning-heavy tasks first.

Depth Probe An LLM app is too slow and expensive. What levers do you pull?

In rough order of ROI: (1) Smaller/faster model for easy requests — route only hard ones to the big model. (2) Prompt caching — reuse the computed prefix for repeated system prompts; providers discount cached tokens heavily. (3) Trim the prompt — shorter context = linear cost savings. (4) Streaming — perceived latency drops. (5) Batching & KV-cache reuse on self-hosted inference (vLLM). (6) Quantize self-hosted models. (7) Cache whole responses for repeated queries (semantic caching).

Mid What is Mixture of Experts (MoE)?

Instead of one big feed-forward block, an MoE layer holds many parallel "expert" blocks and a small router that sends each token to only 1–2 of them. Result: huge total parameter count, but only a fraction active per token — Mixtral 8×7B has ~47B parameters but runs ~13B worth of compute per token. That's the appeal: big-model quality at small-model inference cost. Most frontier models are now MoE-based.

Mid What are MQA and GQA, and why do they exist?

They shrink the KV cache — the memory that grows with every token and limits serving throughput. Standard multi-head attention stores separate keys/values for every head. MQA (Multi-Query Attention): all heads share one K/V set — cache shrinks ~h-fold, small quality hit. GQA (Grouped Query Attention): middle ground — heads share K/V in groups — nearly MQA's memory savings at nearly full quality. Llama 3 and most modern LLMs use GQA.

Surface Which benchmarks and metrics are used to evaluate LLMs?

Capability benchmarks: MMLU (broad knowledge, 57 subjects), HumanEval (code — Pass@k), GSM8K (math word problems), MT-Bench/arena-style Elo for chat quality. Text-overlap metrics: BLEU (precision-oriented, translation), ROUGE (recall-oriented, summarization), BERTScore (semantic similarity via embeddings — survives paraphrasing). Honest caveat: public benchmarks leak into training data over time, so teams increasingly rely on private eval sets and LLM-as-judge.

Mid Beyond CoT — what are self-consistency and Tree-of-Thought?

Self-consistency: sample the same CoT prompt several times and take the majority-vote answer — different reasoning paths converging on one answer is strong evidence it's right; typically adds 5–20% on math/reasoning. Tree-of-Thought: branch into multiple partial reasoning steps, evaluate which branches look promising, expand the best and backtrack from dead ends — search over reasoning. Also name prompt chaining: splitting a task into sequential LLM calls.

Depth Probe Walk me through fine-tuning Llama 3 8B on company data, end to end.

(1) Data: 5–20k instruction→response pairs in chat format; clean PII; split 90/5/5. Quality beats quantity. (2) Method: QLoRA — 4-bit quantized frozen base + LoRA adapters (r=16–64) via HuggingFace PEFT/TRL — fits on a single A100 or RTX 4090. (3) Training: LR ~1e-4 with warmup, 3–5 epochs, early stopping on validation loss. Watch for catastrophic forgetting; mitigate with lower LR and mixing in general instruction data. (4) Evaluate: held-out set with LLM-as-judge + human review, plus a general-capability check. (5) Ship: merge adapters, serve via vLLM, or export GGUF for CPU/edge.

GANs, VAEs & Diffusion

Mid How does a GAN work, and what is mode collapse?

Two networks in a game: a generator turns random noise into fake samples, a discriminator tries to tell fake from real. They train against each other — the generator improves until fakes fool the discriminator. Famous failure: mode collapse — the generator finds a few outputs that reliably fool the discriminator and produces only those, ignoring the diversity of the real data. GAN training is notoriously unstable, which is a key reason diffusion models displaced GANs for image generation.

Mid What is a VAE and what's the reparameterization trick?

A Variational Autoencoder compresses inputs into a probability distribution in latent space (a mean and variance, not a single point), then decodes samples from it back to data. Loss = reconstruction error + a KL term keeping the latent space close to a standard Gaussian — making the space smooth. The reparameterization trick: you can't backprop through random sampling, so rewrite z = μ + σ·ε (with ε ~ N(0,1)) — gradients flow through μ and σ.

Mid How do diffusion models work, and why did they beat GANs?

Forward process: gradually add noise to a real image over many steps until it's pure static. Training: teach a network (usually a U-Net) to predict the noise added at each step. Generation: start from pure noise and iteratively denoise, step by step, into a new image. Why they won: (1) stable training — it's a simple regression loss, no adversarial game; (2) full mode coverage — no mode collapse, better diversity; (3) state-of-the-art quality.

Depth Probe Explain how Stable Diffusion generates an image from a text prompt.

Four pieces: (1) A text encoder (CLIP) turns the prompt into embeddings. (2) The diffusion runs in a VAE's compressed latent space (~64×64 instead of 512×512 pixels) — this "latent diffusion" is the efficiency trick that made it run on consumer GPUs. (3) A U-Net iteratively denoises a random latent, attending to the text embeddings via cross-attention so the image matches the prompt. (4) Classifier-free guidance: each step predicts noise twice — with and without the prompt — and extrapolates toward the prompted direction. Finally the VAE decoder upscales the clean latent.

RAG & Chunking

The AI Engineer interview core

If the JD says "AI Engineer" or mentions LLMs, you WILL get chunking and retrieval questions. Architecture deep-dive: Pillar 5 · GenAI Stack.

Surface What is RAG and why do we need it?

Retrieval-Augmented Generation: before the LLM answers, fetch the most relevant pieces of your own data and paste them into the prompt as context. Why: LLMs don't know your private/recent data, context windows can't hold everything, and fine-tuning for every document update is impractical. Pipeline: ingest → chunk → embed → store in vector DB → retrieve top-K → re-rank → stuff into prompt → generate.

Surface What is chunking and why is it needed?

Chunking is splitting documents into smaller pieces before embedding them. It's needed because: (1) embedding models have input limits, (2) retrieval precision — you want to fetch the exact paragraph that answers the question, not the whole PDF, (3) context budget — small relevant chunks let more distinct sources fit into the prompt. Chunking quality is often the biggest lever in RAG performance.

Mid What are the types of chunking?

Know these six, from simplest to smartest:

1. Fixed-size chunking — cut every N tokens/characters (e.g. 500 tokens). Simple, fast; but cuts sentences mid-thought.

2. Sliding window (overlap) chunking — fixed-size, but consecutive chunks share an overlap (10–20%), so a sentence cut at a boundary survives intact in the neighboring chunk.

3. Recursive chunking — split by a priority list of separators: paragraphs first, then sentences, then words, only cutting deeper when a piece is still too big. The practical default (LangChain's RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter).

4. Document-based / structure-aware chunking — split along the document's own structure: Markdown headers, HTML sections, code functions/classes. Best when documents have reliable structure.

5. Semantic chunking — embed sentences and start a new chunk where the topic shifts (embedding similarity between adjacent sentences drops). Chunks = coherent ideas; costs more compute.

6. Agentic / LLM-based chunking — ask an LLM to split the document the way a human would. Highest quality, highest cost.

Interview-safe recommendation: start with recursive chunking at ~300–512 tokens with 10–15% overlap, evaluate retrieval quality, then get fancier only if needed.

Mid What's the "K" in RAG / chunking retrieval (top-K)?

K = how many of the highest-scoring chunks you retrieve from the vector store and hand to the LLM (typically K = 3–10). Small K: cheaper, less noise, but might miss the chunk that holds the answer. Large K: better recall, but more cost/latency and irrelevant chunks distract the model. Common production pattern: retrieve a generous K (20–50), then re-rank and keep the top 3–5. Note: the same "K" convention appears everywhere in ML — it always just means "how many."

Mid How do you choose chunk size and overlap?

Precision vs context tradeoff. Small chunks (100–300 tokens): precise retrieval but little surrounding context. Large chunks (800–1500+): rich context but blurry retrieval. Sensible starting point: ~300–512 tokens with 10–20% overlap. Adjust by content type: FAQs → small; legal prose → larger; code → split by function. Power move: parent-document retrieval — embed small chunks for precise search, but hand the LLM the larger parent section for context.

Surface What is a vector database and how does it search so fast?

A database that stores embeddings and answers "which stored vectors are closest to this query vector?" It uses ANN (Approximate Nearest Neighbor) indexes — most famously HNSW, a multi-layer graph you navigate greedily from coarse to fine, finding near-neighbors in logarithmic-ish time at ~95–99% recall. Names: Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, Milvus, Chroma, pgvector (Postgres extension — often the pragmatic choice), FAISS (library).

Mid Cosine similarity vs dot product vs Euclidean distance?

Cosine similarity measures the angle between vectors — direction only, magnitude ignored; the default for text embeddings. Dot product = cosine × magnitudes — magnitude matters, used when embeddings are trained for it. Euclidean = straight-line distance. Key practical fact: on normalized (unit-length) vectors, all three rank results identically — match the metric your embedding model was trained with.

Mid Dense vs sparse retrieval, and what is hybrid search?

Sparse (keyword): BM25/TF-IDF — matches exact words. Great for IDs, error codes, names; blind to synonyms. Dense (semantic): embeddings — matches meaning; great for paraphrases, but can fumble exact strings. Hybrid search runs both and merges the ranked lists — typically with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF). This is the production default because real queries mix both needs.

Mid What is re-ranking and why add it?

First-stage retrieval (bi-encoder) embeds the query and documents separately — fast but coarse. A re-ranker (cross-encoder) takes the query and each candidate chunk together through one model and scores true relevance — far more accurate, but too slow to run on millions of docs. So: retrieve top 20–100 fast, re-rank, keep the best 3–5. Typically the single biggest precision boost you can add to a RAG stack.

Depth Probe How do you evaluate a RAG system?

Evaluate the two stages separately. Retrieval metrics: recall@K (is the gold chunk in the top K?), MRR/nDCG. Generation metrics: faithfulness/groundedness (every claim supported by retrieved context — catches hallucination), answer relevance, context relevance. Usually use LLM-as-judge (frameworks: RAGAS, TruLens). Debugging rule: if faithfulness is high but answers are wrong → fix retrieval; if retrieval is right but answers are wrong → fix the prompt/model.

Depth Probe Common RAG failure modes and fixes?

(1) Right answer not retrieved → better chunking, hybrid search, query rewriting, increase K + re-rank. (2) Retrieved but ignored → "lost in the middle"; put the best chunks first/last, retrieve fewer. (3) Question spans multiple documents → decompose the query into sub-questions, retrieve per sub-question. (4) Stale index → scheduled/event-driven re-indexing. (5) Chunks lack context → contextual retrieval: prepend document title/summary to each chunk before embedding.

Mid What are query transformations — HyDE, multi-query, step-back?

Techniques that rewrite the user's query before retrieval, because terse user queries embed poorly. Multi-query: LLM generates 3–5 rephrasings, retrieve for all, merge results. HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings): LLM writes a hypothetical answer to the question, and you embed that for search — a fake answer looks more like the real answer-containing chunk than the question does. Step-back prompting: abstract the specific question to its general principle. Query decomposition: split multi-part questions into sub-queries.

Depth Probe What is GraphRAG and when is it worth it over vector RAG?

GraphRAG builds a knowledge graph from your corpus at ingestion: an LLM extracts entities and relationships, communities of related entities are detected (Leiden algorithm), and each community gets an LLM-written summary. It wins where vector RAG structurally fails: multi-hop questions (the answer spans many documents connected by relationships) and global questions ("what are the main themes across all these reports?" — no single chunk contains the answer). Honest recommendation: hybrid vector RAG covers most use cases; reach for GraphRAG when questions are genuinely relational/aggregative.

Agentic AI

Surface What makes an AI system an "agent" and not just an LLM call?

An agent runs a loop: the LLM decides an action → executes a tool (search, code, API) → observes the result → decides the next action → repeats until the goal is done. A plain LLM call is one-shot text-in/text-out. The defining ingredients: tool use, a feedback loop, and autonomy over the path. A workflow with hardcoded steps that happens to call an LLM twice is a pipeline, not an agent — good interviews reward knowing that distinction.

Mid Explain the ReAct pattern.

Reasoning + Acting, interleaved. Each turn the model produces: Thought ("I need the 2024 revenue — I should search") → Action (search("Acme 2024 revenue")) → Observation (the tool's result) → next Thought… until it emits a final answer. The explicit reasoning step makes tool choice better and the whole trajectory debuggable — you can read exactly where its logic went wrong.

Mid How does function/tool calling actually work?

You send the model a list of tool definitions — name, description, and a JSON Schema of parameters. When the model decides a tool is needed, it outputs a structured request like {"name": "get_weather", "arguments": {"city": "Pune"}}. Your code executes the real function and sends the result back as a new message; the model then continues with that information. Key insight: the model only ever requests calls — execution, validation, and permissions are entirely your responsibility.

Surface What is MCP (Model Context Protocol)?

An open standard (from Anthropic, 2024, since adopted broadly) for connecting AI apps to tools and data. Instead of every app writing custom integrations for every service, a service exposes one MCP server (tools, resources, prompts) and any MCP-capable client can use it. Think "USB-C for AI integrations": one connector standard, N×M integrations become N+M. Servers expose tools (functions), resources (data), and prompts (templates).

Mid How do agents handle memory?

Two layers. Short-term = the context window: the current conversation and tool results; managed by trimming or summarizing older turns when it fills up. Long-term = external storage the agent reads/writes across sessions: typically a vector store of past interactions retrieved by similarity, plus structured stores like user-preference files or knowledge graphs.

Mid When do multi-agent systems beat a single agent?

When the task exceeds one context window or benefits from separation of concerns: parallel research over many sources, a generator + independent critic/reviewer (adversarial checking catches errors self-review misses), or specialists with different tools/prompts (orchestrator-worker pattern). Costs to acknowledge: multiplied token spend, coordination complexity. Honest answer: "single agent until it demonstrably can't" signals engineering maturity.

Depth Probe How do you make an agent safe and reliable in production?

(1) Bound autonomy: max iterations/budget caps, restricted tool permissions (read-only by default), human approval gates for irreversible actions (payments, deletes, emails). (2) Sandbox execution — code tools run in isolated environments. (3) Guard against prompt injection — treat all retrieved/tool-returned content as untrusted data, not instructions; this is the #1 agent-specific attack. (4) Observability — log full trajectories. (5) Evals — a suite of scripted tasks with success criteria, run on every prompt/model change.

Surface Agentic RAG vs plain RAG?

Plain RAG is a fixed pipeline: retrieve once → answer. Agentic RAG gives the model control over retrieval: it can rewrite the query, choose which source/index to search, retrieve multiple times, check whether the results actually answer the question, and search again if not. Better for multi-hop and ambiguous questions; costs more latency and tokens.

Mid Name the main agent design patterns beyond ReAct.

Plan-and-Execute: draft the full step plan up front, then execute — better for long-horizon tasks. Reflexion / self-critique: generate → critique own output → revise; a second pass catches errors. Supervisor (orchestrator-worker): a router agent decomposes the task and delegates to specialist sub-agents, then synthesizes. Handoffs / swarm: decentralized — each agent can transfer the conversation to a peer. Interview answer that lands: pick the simplest pattern that fits.

LangChain & Frameworks

JDs name these explicitly

"LangChain/LangGraph experience" appears verbatim in AI/ML Engineer JDs. Know what each framework is for and when you'd skip them entirely.

Mid LangChain vs LlamaIndex vs LangGraph — which for what?

LangChain: general-purpose LLM plumbing — chains, prompts, tool bindings, integrations with everything; fastest way to prototype. LlamaIndex: specialized for the data side of RAG — ingestion, indexing, and retrieval strategies (parent-child/small-to-big retrieval is first-class). LangGraph: stateful agent orchestration as an explicit graph — nodes, conditional edges, loops, checkpointing, human-in-the-loop interrupts; the most production-ready of the three for agents. Mature answer: for a simple RAG API, direct SDK calls are easier to debug; frameworks earn their complexity when you need their orchestration.

Mid What memory types does LangChain offer for a long conversation?

Ordered by sophistication: Buffer — keep everything (fails at context limits); BufferWindow — keep the last k turns; Summary — LLM-summarize history as it grows; SummaryBuffer — the production default: verbatim recent turns + summary of older ones; VectorStoreRetrieverMemory — embed past turns, retrieve only relevant ones per query; Entity memory — track facts per entity. For a 20-turn support conversation: SummaryBuffer + entity memory is the standard combo.

Surface CrewAI vs AutoGen — the other multi-agent frameworks?

CrewAI: role-based teams — you define agents by role/goal/backstory ("Researcher", "Writer", "Reviewer") and run them sequentially or hierarchically; the most approachable mental model. AutoGen (Microsoft): event-driven actor model — agents exchange async messages, strong at code-execution workflows. One-liner: CrewAI for readable role-play teams, AutoGen for async/code-heavy workflows, LangGraph for controlled production graphs.

Mid How do you stream LLM responses to users, and why does it matter?

Streaming sends tokens as they're generated instead of waiting for the full response. It's the single biggest perceived-latency win: time-to-first-token drops from many seconds to under one, even though total time is unchanged. Mechanics: the LLM API yields chunks (async iterator) → your backend forwards them via SSE (Server-Sent Events) or WebSocket (FastAPI's StreamingResponse) → the UI renders incrementally. Wrinkle: structured/JSON output needs partial-parse handling.

MLOps & Deployment

Surface What are the main ways to serve a model?

Batch: score everything on a schedule (nightly churn scores) — simplest, no latency pressure. Online/real-time: model behind an API, responds per request (fraud check at checkout) — needs low latency, autoscaling, monitoring. Streaming: score events as they flow through a queue (Kafka). Edge/on-device: model ships inside the app — privacy and offline wins, but size-constrained. Choose by: how fresh must predictions be, and at what request rate?

Mid Data drift vs concept drift?

Data drift: the inputs change distribution — new demographics sign up, a sensor recalibrates. The X changed. Concept drift: the relationship between inputs and target changes — the same features now mean something different (spending patterns pre- vs post-pandemic). The P(y|X) changed. Detection: monitor input distributions (PSI, KL divergence) for data drift; monitor live model performance against delayed ground-truth labels for concept drift.

Mid What do you monitor for a deployed model?

Four layers: (1) System health — latency, throughput, error rate, cost. (2) Input data quality — schema violations, missing-value spikes, drift scores per feature. (3) Prediction behavior — output distribution shifts, confidence trends. (4) Business performance — actual accuracy once delayed labels arrive, plus the downstream KPI. For LLM apps add: token cost per request, hallucination/groundedness sampling, refusal rate, and user feedback signals.

Surface When and how do you retrain a model?

Triggers: on a schedule (weekly/monthly), on drift alerts, or on performance drop below a threshold. The retraining pipeline should be fully automated: fetch fresh data → validate → train → evaluate against the current champion model on a held-out set → deploy only if better (champion/challenger), ideally behind a canary rollout. Always keep the old model warm for instant rollback.

Mid Shadow deployment vs canary vs A/B — rolling out a new model?

Shadow: new model receives real traffic and its predictions are logged but never shown to users — zero risk, validates behavior at scale first. Canary: new model serves a small slice (1–5%) of real traffic; watch metrics, expand gradually, auto-rollback on regression. A/B test: statistically rigorous comparison on the business metric with proper randomization. Mature rollout: shadow → canary → A/B → full.

Surface Why version models and data, not just code?

Because a model's behavior = code + data + hyperparameters + preprocessing — reproduce a bug or audit a decision and you need all four as they were. Tools: a model registry (MLflow, W&B) stores each trained model with its metrics, lineage and stage; data versioning (DVC, lakehouse snapshots) pins the exact training set. Also required for compliance ("why was this loan denied?") and for safe rollbacks.

Depth Probe What is a feature store and what problem does it solve?

A central place to define a feature once and serve it both for training (historical, batch) and inference (live, low-latency). The problem it kills: training-serving skew — the feature is computed one way in the offline training pipeline and slightly differently in the online service, so the model sees subtly different inputs in production and quietly underperforms. Also enables feature reuse across teams and point-in-time correct training joins (no future leakage).

Mid What does CI/CD look like for an ML model (vs normal software)?

Everything from software CI/CD (lint, unit tests, build) plus ML-specific gates: (1) data validation — schema, ranges, null rates (Great Expectations) — bad data fails the pipeline before training; (2) reproducible training — pinned data version + code version + config; (3) evaluation gate — new model must beat the current champion by a margin, or the pipeline stops; (4) staged rollout — staging → canary → full, with auto-rollback.

Depth Probe Model hit 94% in training but 81% in production. Diagnose it.

Work through causes in likelihood order: (1) Data leakage in training — preprocessing stats computed before the split, target-derived features, duplicates across splits — making the 94% a lie; re-audit the pipeline first. (2) Training-serving skew — features computed differently in the serving path than in training (a feature store fixes it). (3) Distribution shift — production inputs differ from training data. (4) Concept drift — the world changed since the training snapshot. The structure of the answer matters: say you'd verify each hypothesis with data, not guess-and-retrain.

Serving, Docker & Cloud

The engineering half of "AI/ML Engineer"

FastAPI, Docker, Kubernetes and one cloud platform appear in nearly every AI/ML Engineer JD.

Mid Key patterns for serving a model behind a FastAPI endpoint?

(1) Load the model once at startup (lifespan handler), never per-request. (2) Pydantic models for request/response — free validation and docs. (3) Inference is CPU/GPU-bound, so don't block the async event loop — run it in an executor or use sync endpoints with multiple workers. (4) /health endpoint for Kubernetes probes. (5) Auth + rate limiting at the edge. (6) Long jobs → background tasks/queue, return a job ID. (7) Metrics middleware (Prometheus) for latency P99.

Mid What's specific about Dockerizing an ML/GPU workload?

ML images are huge (PyTorch + CUDA ≈ 10 GB+), so: multi-stage builds, strict .dockerignore, and mount model weights as volumes rather than baking them into the image. GPU access needs the NVIDIA Container Toolkit (--gpus all) and a CUDA-matched base image. PyTorch DataLoaders need --shm-size raised. Add a HEALTHCHECK, run as non-root, pin every version. The theme: images should be reproducible and small; artifacts and data live outside.

Mid What does Kubernetes give an ML service, and which pieces matter?

Self-healing, scaling, and rollout management for containers. Pieces that matter for ML: Deployment (declares replicas + image), Service (stable network name), readiness probe with a long initial delay — models take a minute to load, traffic must not arrive early — HPA (autoscale on CPU or custom metric), GPU scheduling via node selectors and taints (GPU nodes are expensive — keep CPU workloads off them), and Secrets/ConfigMaps for keys.

Mid How do you roll out a new model version with zero downtime?

RollingUpdate with maxSurge=1 / maxUnavailable=0: Kubernetes starts a new pod, waits until its readiness probe passes (model fully loaded), shifts traffic, then kills an old pod — repeat. The probe is the whole trick: a pod that reports ready before the model loads serves errors. For model changes you usually want canary on top: route 5% to the new version, watch error rate and latency, ramp gradually (Argo Rollouts automates this).

Depth Probe What makes vLLM fast? Explain PagedAttention and continuous batching.

Two ideas. PagedAttention: the KV cache normally requires one big contiguous memory block per request, reserved for the maximum possible length — most of it wasted. vLLM manages the cache in small fixed-size pages, like an OS manages virtual memory — fragmentation drops from ~60–80% waste to under 4%, so far more requests fit on the GPU at once. Continuous batching: instead of waiting for a whole batch to finish, finished sequences leave and new requests join the batch every step — the GPU never idles. Together: several-fold throughput gains.

Mid What is speculative decoding?

A small, fast draft model proposes the next several tokens; the big model verifies them all in one forward pass (verification is parallel, generation isn't). Accepted tokens are kept; the first rejected one is corrected by the big model, and drafting resumes from there. Because the acceptance math preserves the big model's exact output distribution, you get 2–3× faster generation with identical quality — no accuracy trade-off.

Mid Serverless (Lambda) vs containers (ECS/Kubernetes) for AI services?

Lambda/serverless: pay-per-invocation, scales to zero, but 15-min runtime and ~10 GB memory limits, no GPU, and cold starts hurt large models. Right for: preprocessing, event triggers (new file in S3 → kick off ingestion), glue code, light CPU inference. Containers (ECS/Fargate/K8s): persistent processes, GPUs, big memory, no time limits — right for model servers, streaming/stateful agents. Production shape: Lambda for the event-driven edges, containers for the model core.

Responsible AI & Governance

Mid How do you handle PII in an AI application?

Layered: (1) Detect — NER-based scanners (spaCy, Microsoft Presidio, AWS Comprehend) find names, emails, phone numbers, IDs. (2) Redact or pseudonymize before the LLM sees it — replace with placeholders ("[CUSTOMER_NAME]"), optionally re-insert after generation. (3) Exclude PII from logs, embeddings, and fine-tuning data — a model fine-tuned on raw support chats can memorize and regurgitate customer data. Regulations: GDPR (EU), HIPAA (US healthcare), the EU AI Act.

Mid How do you detect and measure bias in a model?

Slice performance by sensitive attribute (gender, age, region) and compare group metrics. Two standard fairness definitions — which conflict: demographic parity (equal positive-prediction rates across groups) and equal opportunity (equal true-positive rates — qualified candidates approved at the same rate). You generally can't satisfy both; the right one depends on the harm you're preventing. Tools: IBM AIF360, Fairlearn; for LLMs, bias benchmarks like BBQ and WinoBias.

Surface What's a production guardrail stack for a customer-facing LLM app?

Input side: prompt-injection screening, topic filters (stay on-domain), PII redaction. Output side: toxicity/safety classifiers (Llama Guard, OpenAI Moderation API), groundedness check against retrieved context, format validation, competitor/pricing-claim filters. Process side: red-teaming before launch, model cards documenting intended use and limits, human review queues for flagged outputs, and audit logs of every prompt/response. Key framing: guardrails are layers — no single filter is reliable, so you stack them.

Depth Probe A healthcare client needs an LLM feature. What changes vs a normal deployment?

(1) Compliance boundary first: HIPAA-eligible services only, BAAs with every vendor touching PHI — including the LLM API provider; otherwise self-host. (2) Data minimization: de-identify before inference where possible; no PHI in logs, traces, or third-party analytics. (3) Human-in-the-loop is non-negotiable for anything diagnosis-adjacent — the model drafts, a clinician approves. (4) Provenance: answers must cite sources (RAG over approved clinical content). (5) Audit everything — who asked what, what was retrieved, what was answered, immutably. (6) Extra rigorous evals for hallucinated dosages, missed contraindications.

These are the openers. Interviewers escalate from any of them — when they do, you want the derivations and internals from the six pillars: Math · Classical ML · Deep Learning · Transformers · GenAI Stack · System Design.
